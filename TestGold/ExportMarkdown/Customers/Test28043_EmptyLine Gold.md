1. # **<a name="_toc125048673"></a>Overview/Purpose**
This process will provide guidelines on the detail of information required when authoring a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) and/or Process Description (PD) document.

1. # <a name="_toc125048674"></a>**Scope**
This PD will cover the following document types: -

- Standard Operating Procedures
- Process Descriptions 
- Process
- Policy

1. # <a name="_toc125048675"></a>**Definitions**

**Process Descriptions:**

**Process**

A Process is an in-depth level description of a task or series of tasks. It gives the big picture and overall workflow of a multi-step task. That is**,** describe, dictate or stipulate the steps that must be followed to correctly perform any specific task.

**Policy**

A policy is a set of rules or guidelines that are followed in order to achieve a specific goal (i.e., compliance). Policies answer questions about what employees do and why they do it. 

**Standard Operating Procedure:**

A standard operating procedure is a set of written instructions that describe the tasks that must be taken to properly perform a routine activity and “sit” above the PDs. 

SOPs should be followed the exact same way every time to guarantee that the organization remains consistent and in [compliance](https://www.techtarget.com/searchdatamanagement/definition/compliance) with industry regulations and business standards.

1. # **<a name="_toc125048677"></a>Guidelines**
   1. ## <a name="_toc125048678"></a>**Standard Operating Procedure**

A SOP can be a counterpart to a policy and can be referred to in the policy.

A SOP can be a counterpart to a process and can be referred to in the process.

The following considerations must be made when authoring a SOP: -

- SOPs should be written from a purely practical perspective from the point-of-view from those who will actually use them.
- An effective SOP clearly explains the tasks taken to complete a procedure.
- The SOP should be brief and easy to understand, with a focus on how things should be done rather than what needs to be done (this is covered by the PD - Process)
- Key information that should be detailed in the SOP include: -
- Overview / Purpose
- Why is the SOP required.
- Scope
- What will the SOP cover.
- Definitions
- Clarification of terminology, including acronyms and phrases that may not be familiar to the SOP audience.
- Responsibilities
- What “Roles” are responsible for application of the SOP
- What is expected of the named “Roles”.
- Procedures
- Descriptions of what should be done.
- Discussion of decisions that must be made.
- Security Controls
- Any applicable Health and Safety requirements
- Training Requirements.
- References
- Listing of any applicable regulatory requirements
- ` `Listing of any dependencies, it is possible that the SOP relates to other documents, such as PD.

- The following should be avoided when authoring a SOP: -
- How does a particular function work and what happens, this is covered by PD-Process.



1. ## <a name="_toc125048679"></a>**Process Description – Process**

A PD can be a counterpart to a policy and can be referred to in the policy.

A PD can be a counterpart to a SOP and can be referred to in the SOP.

The following considerations must be made when authoring a Process: -

- The process should be written from a purely practical perspective from the point-of-view from those who will actually use them.
- Key information that should be detailed in the process include: -
- Overview / Purpose
- Why is the process required.
- Scope
- What will the process cover.
- Definitions
- Clarification of terminology, including acronyms and phrases that may not be familiar to the process audience.
- Responsibilities
- What “Roles” are responsible for application of the process
- What is expected of the named “Roles”.
- Process
- What needs to be accomplished and how it will be executed.
- Describe each step in detail, including all the elements needed to complete the step.
- Who performs what action.
- Where the inputs come from and where the outputs go.
- Any locational requirements (i.e., where an activity is performed).
- Tools, information or other resources required.
- Descriptions of what needs to be done.
- Discussion of decisions that must be made.
- References
- Listing of any dependencies, it is possible that the PD relates to other documents, such as SOP.


- The following should be avoided when authoring a Process: -
- Too many technical terms (without explaining them)
- Don’t use different words for the same thing. For example, if you use the term “customer” then only use that throughout the whole document.

  1. ## <a name="_toc125048680"></a>**Process Description – Policy**

The following considerations must be made when authoring a Policy: -

- Write a policy in clear and simple terms. This is because employees might give individual interpretations of the policy, which might be different from what the policy intended to pass along. This could lead to misguided actions that may affect the organization adversely.
- The author needs to ensure that there is a clear statement as to why the policy has been created, such as changes in law, regulations, standards, compliance guidance, etc. This can be used to introduce the context of the policy document. 
- If the policy relates to a specific law, regulation or compliance standard, the author needs to explain how the policy document is designed to address that issue. This will assist covered persons’ understanding and application of the policy.
- The policy shall explain the range of application of the policy in terms of covered persons, facilities, sites, etc
- Key information that should be detailed in the Policy include: -
- Overview / Purpose
- Why is the policy required.
- Scope
- Who needs to follow the policy.
- Definitions
- Clarification of terminology, including acronyms and phrases that may not be familiar to the policy audience.
- Responsibilities
- What “Roles” are responsible for application of the policy
- What is expected of the named “Roles”.
- Policy
- Descriptions of what needs to be done.
- What are the major conditions or restrictions (if any).
- Are there exclusions or special situations where the policy does not apply.
- References
- Listing of any applicable regulatory requirements
- Listing of any dependencies, it is possible that the Policy relates to other documents, such as SOP and PD.




- The following should be avoided when authoring a Policy: -
- Using any data that might easily get outdated.
- Including background details on the policy nor should it contain procedural steps.
- Using a specific label, such as the name of a software product.

1. ## <a name="_toc125048681"></a>**Instructions**

The following instructions must be followed when authoring a PD / SOP: -

- **Template:** Always ensure that the current approved PD and SOP templates in eProf are used.
- **Write concisely, clearly, and follow a step-by-step format:** Keep sentences as brief as possible and use simple, common terms. Never obfuscate the meaning of an instruction by using overly technical or jargon-filled terminology when a simpler, clearer word or phrase communicates the same idea.
- **Write in the active voice and present the main idea first:** Be mindful of what you write as well as *how you write it*. Simple action-oriented verbs such as "identify," "direct," "evaluate," and "review" get the point across without requiring interpretation. If at all possible, do not use the passive voice when structuring sentences as this has been shown to confuse and misdirect attention away from important ideas.
- **Avoid ambiguity:** Always avoid using generalized terms that give no tangible meaning. Words like "periodic" "typical" "general" and "should" do not enforce any consistent direction or execution of a directive — the main goal of having an PD / SOP in the first place.
- **Be careful around important terms:** The main terms here are "may," "must," and "should." Remember that using the word "may" gives personnel decision-making power and/or flexibility depending on the context. "Must," is always mandatory and "should" is by nature conditional.
- **Make smart use of formatting:** If the PD / SOP consists of long, dense paragraphs, chances are there's a better formatting scheme to follow. Bulleted items and lists are particularly effective for certain pieces of information as they focus attention and slow reader's pace.


1. ## **Creating a new procedure, process, or policy**

When a new document is introduced (procedure, process, or policy), the following steps must be performed:

- upload the document to the internal document management system (location –   Generis Internal->Cross-Product Quality-><sub-folder that corresponds to the type of the document>),
- define the owner of the policy (specify the corresponding property for the uploaded document),
- add the document to the Generis Documents Management Plan,
- update the Training Matrix for the company employees (Generis Job Descriptions) with who should be trained on this document,
- update the Internal Audit Program (if applicable)
- send the document for approval as per the Generis Documents Management Plan.

When the newly introduced document is approved, it should be sent to all the staff as per the Training Matrix.
